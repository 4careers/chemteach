3.6 Electronic Configurations and Types of Elements

Because elements in the same group have similar valence shell electronic configurations, the Periodic Table is naturally divided into four main blocks based on the type of orbital being filled with electrons:

  • s-Block: Groups 1 and 2
  • p-Block: Groups 13 to 18
  • d-Block: Groups 3 to 12 (Transition metals)
  • f-Block: The two bottom rows (Inner transition metals)

Let's look at each block in detail.

3.6.1 The s-Block Elements

The elements of Group 1 (alkali metals) and Group 2 (alkaline earth metals) constitute the s-block.

  • Configuration: ns1 (Group 1) and ns2 (Group 2).
  • Properties: They are highly reactive metals with low ionization enthalpies.
  • Reactivity: They readily lose their outermost electron(s) to form $+1$ (Group 1) or $+2$ (Group 2) ions. Their metallic character and reactivity increase as we go down the group.
  • Occurrence: Because they are so reactive, they are never found pure in nature.

3.6.2 The p-Block Elements

The p-block comprises Groups 13 to 18. Together with the s-block, they are called the Representative Elements or Main Group Elements.

  • Configuration: Varies from ns2 np1 to ns2 np6 in each period.
  • Noble Gases (Group 18): Have a completely filled valence shell (ns2 np6). They are highly stable and unreactive.
  • Halogens (Group 17) & Chalcogens (Group 16): Have high negative electron gain enthalpies and readily gain 1 or 2 electrons to attain a stable noble gas configuration.

Non-metallic character increases across a period (left to right) and decreases down a group.

3.6.3 The d-Block Elements (Transition Elements)

These elements form the center of the Periodic Table (Groups 3 to 12).

  • Configuration: (n-1)d1-10 ns0-2
  • Properties: They are all metals. They mostly form colored ions, exhibit variable oxidation states (valency), and are often used as catalysts.
  • Role: They form a "transition" or bridge between the highly reactive metals of the s-block and the non-metals of the p-block.

3.6.4 The f-Block Elements (Inner-Transition Elements)

These are the two rows placed at the bottom of the Periodic Table.

  • Configuration: (n-2)f1-14 (n-1)d0-1 ns2
  • Lanthanoids: Ce (Z=58) to Lu (Z=71). They have very similar properties to one another.
  • Actinoids: Th (Z=90) to Lr (Z=103). The chemistry of actinoids is complicated because they have many oxidation states and are all radioactive. Elements after Uranium are called transuranium elements.

3.6.5 Metals, Non-metals, and Metalloids

Metals: Comprise over 78% of all elements. Usually solid at room temp (except Hg), high melting/boiling points, good conductors. Located on the left and center of the table.

Non-metals: Located at the top right. Usually gases or brittle solids (except Br), poor conductors, low melting/boiling points (except B and C).

Metalloids (Semi-metals): A diagonal "zig-zag" line (Silicon, Germanium, Arsenic, Antimony, Tellurium) separates metals from non-metals. They show properties characteristic of both.

3.7 Periodic Trends in Properties of Elements

Many observable physical and chemical properties vary systematically with atomic number.

This periodicity arises directly from the periodicity in electronic configurations. For instance, elements with identical valence configurations (i.e., in the same group) exhibit similar properties.

We will examine several key trends: Atomic Radius, Ionic Radius, Ionization Enthalpy, Electron Gain Enthalpy, and Electronegativity. Today, we begin with Atomic Radius.

3.7.1(a) Atomic Radius

An atom does not have a sharp boundary, so measuring its absolute size is impossible. Instead, we measure the distance between the nuclei of bonded atoms.

  • Covalent Radius: Half the distance between two adjacent nuclei of the same element in a covalently bonded molecule (e.g., Cl2).
  • Metallic Radius: Half the internuclear distance separating the metal cores in the metallic crystal (e.g., solid Copper).

We generally refer to both simply as "Atomic Radius." Let's look at how this radius changes across the Periodic Table.

Trends in Atomic Radius

Across a Period (Left to Right)

Atomic radius decreases. Why? As we move across a period, electrons are added to the same principal shell, while protons are added to the nucleus. The effective nuclear charge increases, pulling the entire electron cloud closer to the nucleus.

Down a Group (Top to Bottom)

Atomic radius increases. Why? As we move down, new principal shells (n) are added. The outermost electrons are further from the nucleus, and the inner electrons shield them from the nuclear pull, making the atom larger.

Session Summary

  • 🔹 s and p blocks: Known as Representative Elements. Group 18 is Noble Gases, highly stable.
  • 🔹 d and f blocks: Transition and Inner-Transition metals, bridging highly reactive metals and non-metals.
  • 🔹 Metalloids: The zig-zag line in the p-block dividing metals and non-metals.
  • 🔹 Atomic Radius: Decreases across a period (increasing nuclear charge), increases down a group (new shells added).
