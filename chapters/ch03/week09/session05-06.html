3.7 Periodic Trends (Ionic Radius, Ionization & Electron Gain Enthalpy, Electronegativity)

3.7.1(b) Ionic Radius

The removal or addition of an electron to an atom changes its size.

  • Cation (Positive Ion): Formed by losing an electron. A cation is always smaller than its parent atom because it has fewer electrons but the same number of protons (increasing effective nuclear charge per electron).
  • Anion (Negative Ion): Formed by gaining an electron. An anion is always larger than its parent atom because the added electron increases electron-electron repulsion, causing the cloud to expand.

Isoelectronic Species

Species with the same number of electrons (e.g., $O^{2-}$, $F^-$, $Na^+$, $Mg^{2+}$ all have 10 electrons). Among isoelectronic species, the one with the higher nuclear charge (more protons) has a smaller radius.

3.7.1(c) Ionization Enthalpy ($\Delta_i H$)

The minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from an isolated gaseous atom.

It is always a positive value (endothermic) because energy must be supplied to overcome the nucleus's attraction.

  • Successive Ionization: The second ionization enthalpy ($\Delta_i H_2$) is always higher than the first because it's harder to remove an electron from an already positive ion.

Trends in Ionization Enthalpy

Across a Period

Generally increases from left to right. Why? The effective nuclear charge increases and the atomic radius decreases, making it harder to remove an electron.

Anomaly: Be ($1s^2 2s^2$) > B ($1s^2 2s^2 2p^1$). The fully filled 2s orbital is more stable than the partially filled 2p orbital.

Down a Group

Generally decreases from top to bottom. Why? The outermost electron is further from the nucleus and heavily shielded by inner electrons, making it easier to remove.

3.7.1(d) Electron Gain Enthalpy ($\Delta_{eg} H$)

The enthalpy change when an electron is added to an isolated gaseous atom to form a negative ion.

Unlike ionization enthalpy, this can be exothermic (negative) or endothermic (positive).

  • Halogens (Group 17): Have highly negative $\Delta_{eg} H$ because gaining one electron gives them a stable noble gas configuration.
  • Noble Gases (Group 18): Have highly positive $\Delta_{eg} H$ because the added electron would have to enter a higher principal quantum level, making the process highly unfavorable.

Trends in Electron Gain Enthalpy

Across a period: Becomes more negative (more exothermic) as effective nuclear charge increases.

Down a group: Becomes less negative.

Important Anomaly: The electron gain enthalpy of O or F is less negative than that of the succeeding element (S or Cl). Why? Because O and F are very small atoms. Adding an electron to their compact 2p orbitals causes significant electron-electron repulsion.

3.7.1(e) Electronegativity

A qualitative measure of the ability of an atom in a chemical compound to attract shared electrons to itself.

Unlike ionization enthalpy (which is absolute), electronegativity is a relative scale. The most common scale is the Pauling scale, where Fluorine is assigned the highest value (4.0).

  • Across a period: Increases (due to increasing effective nuclear charge and smaller size).
  • Down a group: Decreases (due to larger atomic size).

4.1 Kossel-Lewis Approach to Chemical Bonding

We've now completed Chapter 3! We know atoms have varying radii, ionization enthalpies, and electronegativities. How does this dictate the way they combine to form molecules?

In 1916, Kossel and Lewis independently proposed a logical explanation for chemical bonding based on the inertness of noble gases.

They realized that noble gases are highly stable because they have a completely filled outer shell of 8 electrons (an octet).

The Octet Rule

Lewis postulated that atoms achieve the stable octet when they are linked by chemical bonds.

The Octet Rule: Atoms can combine either by transfer of valence electrons from one atom to another (gaining or losing) or by sharing of valence electrons in order to have an octet in their valence shells.

  • Kossel's approach: Explained the formation of ionic bonds via electron transfer (e.g., $Na$ loses 1, $Cl$ gains 1).
  • Lewis's approach: Explained the formation of covalent bonds via electron sharing (e.g., $Cl$ shares 1 pair with another $Cl$).

Lewis Dot Structures

Lewis introduced a simple notation to represent valence electrons in an atom, using dots around the atomic symbol.

  • $Li \cdot$
  • $\cdot Be \cdot$
  • $\cdot \dot{C} \cdot$
  • $:\ddot{N}\cdot$

These symbols instantly show the number of valence electrons, which helps calculate the common or group valence of the element (either the number of dots, or 8 minus the number of dots).

Session Summary

  • 🔹 Periodic Trends: Ionization enthalpy and electronegativity increase across a period and decrease down a group. Atomic radius does the opposite.
  • 🔹 Anomalies: Small atoms (Be, N, O, F) often break smooth trends due to orbital stability or electron-electron repulsion.
  • 🔹 Bonding (Chapter 4): Atoms bond to achieve a stable octet, mimicking noble gases, either by transferring electrons (Ionic) or sharing them (Covalent).
