3.4 Nomenclature of Elements with Z > 100

The naming of new elements traditionally belonged to the discoverer. However, finding elements with atomic numbers > 100 is highly competitive.

For example, both American and Soviet scientists claimed credit for discovering element 104. The Americans named it Rutherfordium, while the Soviets named it Kurchatovium.

To avoid such controversies while claims are being verified, the IUPAC (International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry) created a systematic, temporary nomenclature based purely on the element's atomic number.

The Roots for Systematic Naming

The name is derived directly from the atomic number using numerical roots, ending with the suffix -ium.

Example: Element 101 is un + nil + un + ium = Unnilunium (Unu).

3.5 Electronic Configurations and the Periodic Table

An element's location in the Periodic Table reflects the quantum numbers of the last orbital filled.

The Principal Quantum Number (n) defines the main energy level (shell). Crucially, the Period Number corresponds to the highest principal quantum number (n) of the elements in that period.

  • Period 1 elements fill the n=1 shell (1s).
  • Period 2 elements fill the n=2 shell (2s, 2p).
  • Period 3 elements fill the n=3 shell (3s, 3p).

The number of elements in each period is twice the number of atomic orbitals available in the energy level being filled.

3.5.1 Electronic Configurations in Periods (Period 1)

First Period (n = 1):

The lowest energy level is 1s. Since an s-orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, this period has exactly 2 elements.

  • Hydrogen (Z=1): 1s1
  • Helium (Z=2): 1s2 (The first shell is now completely full, making Helium a noble gas).

Periods 2 & 3: Filling the 2nd and 3rd Shells

Second Period (n = 2)

Starts with Lithium (1s2 2s1) and Beryllium (1s2 2s2). Next, the 2p orbitals begin to fill starting with Boron (2s2 2p1) and ending with Neon (2s2 2p6). Total: 8 elements (2 in s, 6 in p).

Third Period (n = 3)

Begins at Sodium (Na). The added electron enters the 3s orbital (3s1). Filling continues identically through the 3p orbitals until Argon (3s2 3p6). Total: 8 elements.

Note: The 3d orbitals are available in the 3rd shell, but according to the Aufbau principle, the 4s orbital is lower in energy than 3d, so 3d doesn't start filling until the 4th period.

Fourth Period (n = 4) and the Transition Series

Begins at Potassium (K), filling the 4s orbital (4s1). Calcium fills the second 4s spot (4s2).

The 3d Transition Series: After 4s is filled, the next lowest energy orbital is the 3d orbital. Scandium (Sc, Z=21) starts this filling: 3d1 4s2. The 3d orbitals (which hold 10 electrons) are filled across the next 10 elements, ending at Zinc (Zn, 3d10 4s2). This forms the first transition series.

After Zinc, the 4p orbitals fill from Gallium to Krypton. Total: 18 elements (2 in 4s + 10 in 3d + 6 in 4p).

Fifth Period (n = 5)

Exactly analogous to the fourth period, the 5s, 4d, and 5p orbitals are filled.

  • Starts with Rubidium (Rb).
  • The 4d orbitals are filled forming the second transition series starting from Yttrium (Y) to Cadmium (Cd).
  • Ends with Xenon (Xe) when the 5p orbitals are completely filled.

Total: 18 elements.

Sixth Period (n = 6) and the Lanthanoid Series

This period introduces a new subshell: the 4f orbitals.

  • Starts with filling the 6s orbital (Cs, Ba).
  • Then, electrons begin to fill the 4f orbitals (which can hold 14 electrons). This forms the Lanthanoid series (or inner transition series) from Cerium (Ce, Z=58) to Lutetium (Lu, Z=71).
  • After 4f, the 5d orbitals fill (third transition series).
  • Finally, the 6p orbitals fill, ending at Radon (Rn).

Total: 32 elements (2 in 6s + 14 in 4f + 10 in 5d + 6 in 6p).

Seventh Period (n = 7) and the Actinoid Series

The seventh period is similarly long and includes the filling of the 7s, 5f, 6d, and 7p orbitals.

Filling the 5f orbitals generates the Actinoid series from Thorium (Th, Z=90) to Lawrencium (Lr, Z=103).

Most elements in the actinoid series are radioactive and artificially synthesized. This period is now complete up to element 118 (Oganesson), fulfilling the maximum capacity of 32 elements.

3.5.2 Groupwise Electronic Configurations

Elements belonging to the same vertical column (Group) exhibit similar chemical properties. Why?

Because they have the exact same number and distribution of electrons in their outermost (valence) shells.

Example: Group 1 (Alkali Metals)

  • Lithium: [He] 2s1
  • Sodium: [Ne] 3s1
  • Potassium: [Ar] 4s1
  • Rubidium: [Kr] 5s1

Therefore, we can classify elements into four major blocks (s, p, d, f) depending on which orbital the last electron enters. We will cover these blocks in detail in the next session!

Session Summary

  • 🔹 Nomenclature (Z>100): Systematic IUPAC naming ensures universal understanding while permanent names are ratified using roots (nil, un, bi, etc.).
  • 🔹 Periods & n: The period number corresponds to the highest principal quantum number (n) being filled.
  • 🔹 Period Lengths: 2, 8, 8, 18, 18, 32, 32 elements respectively. 4th period introduces 3d, 6th introduces 4f.
  • 🔹 Groups: Elements in the same group share identical valence shell configurations, giving them similar properties.
